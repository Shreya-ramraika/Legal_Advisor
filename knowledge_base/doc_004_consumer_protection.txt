CONSUMER PROTECTION ACT, 2019 — COMPLETE GUIDE

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 is a landmark legislation that replaced the older 1986 Act and significantly strengthened the rights of consumers in India. It covers defective goods, deficient services, unfair trade practices, misleading advertisements, and e-commerce transactions. Every Indian who buys goods or avails services is a consumer under this law and is entitled to its protections.

WHO IS A CONSUMER?

Under Section 2(7) of the Act, a consumer is any person who:
- Buys goods for a consideration (payment) which has been paid or promised
- Hires or avails of services for a consideration
- Uses the goods with the approval of the buyer
- Is a beneficiary of services with the approval of the person who hired them

Important exclusion: A person who buys goods for commercial resale or avails services for commercial purposes is NOT a consumer under this Act.

SIX FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS OF CONSUMERS

1. Right to Safety: Protection against goods and services that are hazardous to life and property.
2. Right to Information: Right to know the quality, quantity, potency, purity, standard, and price of goods or services.
3. Right to Choose: Right to access a variety of goods and services at competitive prices.
4. Right to be Heard: Right to be heard and assured that consumer interests will receive due consideration.
5. Right to Redressal: Right to seek redressal against unfair trade practices or restrictive trade practices or exploitation.
6. Right to Consumer Education: Right to acquire the knowledge and skills to be an informed consumer.

WHAT CAN YOU COMPLAIN ABOUT?

Under Section 2(6), the following constitute consumer complaints:
1. Defective goods: Goods that do not meet quality, quantity, or safety standards.
2. Deficient services: Services that fall below the promised or expected standard (includes banking, insurance, hospitals, telecom, construction).
3. Unfair trade practices: Misleading advertisements, false representations about the quality or standard of goods.
4. Restrictive trade practices: Practices that restrict competition or are harmful to consumers.
5. Charging above MRP: Selling at a price higher than the Maximum Retail Price printed on the product.
6. Overcharging or denial of services: Refusing to provide services or charging excessively.

E-COMMERCE AND ONLINE TRANSACTIONS (NEW IN 2019)

The 2019 Act specifically covers e-commerce for the first time. Online marketplaces like Amazon, Flipkart, Meesho, and service providers like Zomato, Urban Company are now explicitly covered. You can file a complaint if:
- A product ordered online is not delivered
- A product is significantly different from what was described
- Refund is denied without valid reason
- Seller makes false claims about the product

THREE-TIER DISPUTE RESOLUTION (CONSUMER FORUMS)

Tier 1 — District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (DCDRC):
- Jurisdiction: Complaints where the value of goods/services and compensation claimed does not exceed Rs. 1 crore
- Where to file: District where you reside, or where the opposite party (seller/service provider) has its office, or where the cause of action arose
- Filing fee: Nominal (ranges from Rs. 100 to Rs. 500 based on claim amount)

Tier 2 — State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (SCDRC):
- Jurisdiction: Complaints where value exceeds Rs. 1 crore but does not exceed Rs. 10 crore
- Also handles appeals from the District Commission

Tier 3 — National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC):
- Jurisdiction: Complaints where value exceeds Rs. 10 crore
- Also handles appeals from the State Commission
- Located in New Delhi

TIME LIMIT TO FILE A COMPLAINT

Under Section 69 of the Act, a complaint must be filed within 2 years from the date on which the cause of action arose. The forum may condone delay if sufficient cause is shown. Do not delay filing — collect all evidence and file promptly.

HOW TO FILE A CONSUMER COMPLAINT

Option A — Online (Recommended):
1. Visit consumerhelpline.gov.in or edaakhil.nic.in
2. Register as a consumer with your mobile number and Aadhaar
3. Fill in the complaint form with details of the opposite party, nature of complaint, and relief sought
4. Upload evidence: bills, receipts, emails, photos, warranty cards
5. Pay the nominal filing fee online
6. You will receive an acknowledgement and case number
7. Track progress online — hearings are now conducted virtually too

Option B — Physical Filing:
Visit the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission in your district. Get the complaint form, fill it in triplicate, attach evidence copies (self-attested), and pay the filing fee at the counter.

WHAT TO INCLUDE IN YOUR COMPLAINT

1. Full name and address of the complainant (you)
2. Full name and address of the opposite party (seller/company)
3. Description of goods purchased or services availed
4. Date of purchase/transaction
5. Nature of defect or deficiency
6. Amount of compensation claimed and basis for it
7. Supporting documents: Invoice, receipt, warranty, correspondence, medical reports (if injury), photos

RELIEFS AVAILABLE TO CONSUMERS

If your complaint is upheld, the forum can order:
- Replacement of defective goods
- Refund of the price paid
- Removal of deficiency in service
- Discontinuation of unfair trade practice
- Compensation for loss or injury suffered (including mental agony)
- Punitive damages in cases of gross negligence
- Interest on the refund amount
- Cost of litigation (advocate fees, travel)

IMPORTANT PROVISIONS IN THE 2019 ACT

Product Liability (Chapter VI): Manufacturers, sellers, and service providers are now strictly liable for harm caused by defective products or deficient services. You do not need to prove negligence — only that the product was defective.

Mediation: The 2019 Act introduced mandatory mediation before adjudication in cases where there is a possibility of settlement. This speeds up resolution significantly.

Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA): A new regulatory body empowered to recall unsafe products, discontinue misleading advertisements, and impose penalties up to Rs. 10 lakh on manufacturers for false advertisements.

Penalties for Misleading Advertisements: Advertisers and endorsers (including celebrities) can be penalised for misleading advertisements.

COMMON COMPLAINTS WHERE CONSUMERS WIN

- Mobile phones, electronics, and appliances with manufacturing defects
- Builder/developer delays in possession of flat or apartment
- Insurance claim rejections without valid reasons
- Hospital overcharging or medical negligence
- Airlines for lost baggage, flight delays, or denied boarding
- Banks for unauthorised charges, credit card fraud, or incorrect EMI deduction
- Telecom companies for billing errors or service disruption

IMPORTANT NOTE

Consumer forums are designed to be accessible to ordinary citizens without requiring a lawyer. You can represent yourself. However, for complex cases involving large amounts or medical negligence, engaging a consumer law advocate is advisable. The National Consumer Helpline number is 1800-11-4000 (toll-free) or 1915. You can also WhatsApp on 8800001915 for consumer grievance redressal.
