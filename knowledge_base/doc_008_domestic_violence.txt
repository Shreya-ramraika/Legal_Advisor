PROTECTION OF WOMEN FROM DOMESTIC VIOLENCE ACT, 2005 — COMPLETE GUIDE

The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA) is a landmark civil law that provides comprehensive protection to women suffering domestic violence. Unlike Section 498A IPC (which is criminal), the PWDVA provides civil remedies including protection orders, residence orders, monetary relief, and custody orders — and is far faster and more accessible for victims than a criminal trial.

WHO IS PROTECTED UNDER THIS LAW?

Any woman who is or has been in a domestic relationship with the respondent (abuser) is protected. This includes:
- Wives or ex-wives
- Women in live-in relationships or relationships in the nature of marriage
- Sisters, mothers, daughters, or other women relatives living in the shared household
- Women in joint families

The respondent (person against whom the complaint is made) can be any male adult member of the household, or in some cases female relatives of the husband.

WHAT IS DOMESTIC VIOLENCE?

Section 3 defines domestic violence broadly to include:

1. Physical Abuse: Any act that causes bodily pain, harm, or danger to life, limb, or health. This includes beating, hitting, slapping, kicking, burning, or any form of physical assault.

2. Sexual Abuse: Any conduct of a sexual nature that abuses, humiliates, degrades, or violates the dignity of the woman.

3. Emotional or Verbal Abuse: Insults, ridicule, humiliation, name-calling, repeated threats to cause physical pain, threats to take away children, calling her a witch or barren.

4. Economic Abuse: Deprivation of financial resources — not providing money for food, clothing, medicines; stopping the woman from working; disposing of her stridhan (gifts and jewellery given to her); taking her salary or income.

5. Dowry Harassment: Demands for dowry and harassment related to such demands (also covered under IPC 498A and Section 304B for dowry death).

This is a much wider definition than what many people assume — domestic violence is NOT just physical beating.

REMEDIES AVAILABLE UNDER PWDVA

Protection Order (Section 18):
The court can prohibit the respondent from:
- Committing any act of domestic violence
- Entering the workplace, school, or any place the victim frequents
- Attempting to communicate with the victim
- Alienating assets the victim has an interest in

Residence Order (Section 19):
The court can order that the victim:
- Continue to reside in the shared household (she cannot be thrown out even if the house is owned by the husband or in-laws)
- The respondent is directed not to disturb the victim's possession of the shared household
- A separate residence may be arranged for the victim at the respondent's expense

Monetary Relief (Section 20):
The court can order the respondent to pay:
- Loss of earnings
- Medical expenses
- Maintenance for the victim and her children
- Loss suffered due to destruction of property
- There is no ceiling on the amount

Custody Orders (Section 21):
The court can grant temporary custody of children to the victim during the proceedings.

Compensation Order (Section 22):
The court can order compensation and damages for injuries caused, including mental torture and emotional distress.

HOW TO FILE A COMPLAINT UNDER PWDVA

Who to approach — you have multiple options:

Option 1 — Protection Officer: Every district has appointed Protection Officers (under Sections 8-9 of the Act). They assist in filing the Domestic Incident Report (DIR) and applying for relief orders. They are often based at women's helpline offices or Social Welfare Department offices.

Option 2 — Police Station: File a complaint at the local police station. The police must register the complaint and refer it to the Protection Officer.

Option 3 — Magistrate's Court (Directly): File an application directly before the Judicial Magistrate First Class in your area. You can do this without a lawyer.

Option 4 — Service Providers: Registered NGOs and women's organisations recognised under PWDVA can assist in filing complaints and obtaining relief.

Step-by-Step Process:
1. File a Domestic Incident Report (DIR) with the Protection Officer or police.
2. The Protection Officer assists in obtaining emergency relief and files an application before the Magistrate.
3. The Magistrate can pass an ex parte (one-sided, without hearing the respondent) interim protection order within 24 to 48 hours in urgent situations.
4. The respondent is served notice and given an opportunity to be heard.
5. Final orders are passed after hearing both parties.
6. Violation of any order is punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year and/or fine up to Rs. 20,000.

INTERACTION WITH IPC SECTION 498A

Section 498A IPC makes cruelty by husband or his relatives towards a wife a criminal offence punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment. The PWDVA and Section 498A can be pursued simultaneously. The PWDVA provides civil relief (protection, maintenance, residence), while Section 498A results in criminal prosecution. Many women file both simultaneously for maximum protection.

IMPORTANT RIGHTS

- A woman cannot be evicted from the shared household while proceedings are pending.
- The protection officer is required to assist the victim free of charge.
- Legal aid is available free at the DLSA.
- The proceedings are not publicised and records may be kept confidential to protect the victim's identity.

If you are in immediate danger, call 112 (national emergency number) for immediate police assistance.

IMPORTANT NOTE

You do not need to be physically beaten to file a complaint under PWDVA. Economic abuse, verbal abuse, and emotional torture are all domestic violence under this law. You do not need your husband's or family's permission to file a complaint. Your complaint cannot be withdrawn under pressure — the Magistrate ensures this. You are not alone. Free legal aid and shelter homes (Swadhar Greh) are available in every district under the Ministry of Women and Child Development.
