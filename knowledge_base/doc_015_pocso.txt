PROTECTION OF CHILDREN FROM SEXUAL OFFENCES ACT, 2012 (POCSO) — COMPLETE GUIDE

The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012 (POCSO Act) is a comprehensive law designed to protect children below the age of 18 from sexual abuse and exploitation. It was significantly strengthened by the 2019 Amendment, which introduced the death penalty for aggravated penetrative sexual assault on children below 12 years. POCSO establishes special courts, mandates a child-friendly investigation process, and places the burden of proof on the accused rather than the child victim.

WHO IS A CHILD UNDER POCSO?

Under Section 2(d) of the POCSO Act, a child means any person below the age of eighteen years. There is NO minimum age — the protection applies from birth to 17 years and 364 days. The gender of the child does not matter — both male and female children are protected.

OFFENCES DEFINED UNDER POCSO

The Act defines several categories of sexual offences:

1. Penetrative Sexual Assault (Section 3): Any act of penetration of a sexual nature by a body part or object, or manipulation of the child's body part to cause penetration. Punishment: Imprisonment of 7 to 10 years and fine (minimum 7 years).

2. Aggravated Penetrative Sexual Assault (Section 5): Penetrative sexual assault committed by a person in a position of trust or authority (teacher, family member, doctor, police officer, armed forces), or where it causes grievous hurt, or where the child is below 12 years. Punishment: 10 years to life imprisonment, and fine. Death penalty for offences against children below 12 years (after 2019 Amendment).

3. Sexual Assault (Section 7): Any act with sexual intent involving touching private parts, or making the child touch the accused's body, without penetration. Punishment: 3 to 5 years imprisonment and fine.

4. Aggravated Sexual Assault (Section 9): Sexual assault by persons in trust or authority, or causing grievous hurt. Punishment: 5 to 7 years imprisonment and fine.

5. Sexual Harassment (Section 11): Making sexual remarks, exhibiting genitals, cyberstalking, showing pornography to a child. Punishment: Up to 3 years and fine.

6. Using Child for Pornography (Section 13): Using a child in any medium for sexual gratification or pornographic purposes. Punishment: Imprisonment up to 5 years and fine for first offence; up to 7 years for second offence.

MANDATORY REPORTING — EVERY CITIZEN'S DUTY

Section 19 of the POCSO Act imposes a legal obligation on every person (not just officials) to report an offence or apprehension of an offence under POCSO to the police or the Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU). Failure to report is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months and/or fine.

Who must mandatorily report:
- Anyone who suspects or knows of POCSO offences
- Media persons who come across child pornography while reporting
- Hotels, hospitals, clinics, and educational institutions who have information about such offences

Section 20 obliges media and internet service providers to report child pornographic content immediately to the police.

CHILD-FRIENDLY INVESTIGATION PROCEDURES

POCSO mandates special procedures to protect the dignity and privacy of the child victim throughout the process:

1. Statement Recording: The child's statement must be recorded by a police officer not below the rank of Sub-Inspector, preferably a woman officer, in a place comfortable for the child (not a police station).

2. Medical Examination: The child's medical examination must be conducted by a woman doctor in the presence of a trusted adult. For boys, a male doctor may conduct the examination.

3. No Repeated Examination: The child cannot be subjected to repeated medical examinations. The POCSO Medical Guidelines apply.

4. Magistrate Recording: The child's statement can be recorded by the Magistrate at the child's residence or a place of the child's choice.

5. Video Recording: Wherever possible, statements should be video-recorded.

6. Privacy: No media can publish the name, address, school, or any identifying information of the child victim. Violation is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months and/or fine.

SPECIAL COURTS FOR POCSO

Every district must have a designated Special Court for POCSO cases. Key features:
- Trial must be completed in 1 year (ideally), in a child-friendly environment
- Child gives evidence in camera (in a closed court, without public presence)
- Child does not face the accused directly — a screen or one-way mirror may be used
- A support person (parent, counsellor, or trusted adult) accompanies the child throughout
- No aggressive or suggestive cross-examination of the child is permitted

BURDEN OF PROOF — REVERSED

This is a groundbreaking provision. Under POCSO:
- Section 29: Once the accused is charged, the court presumes the person is guilty unless proven innocent (reverse burden of proof)
- Section 30: If the accused's mental state (knowledge or intention) is relevant, the burden of proving they did NOT have that intent lies on the accused

This is a major protection for child victims who may not have access to sufficient evidence.

HOW TO REPORT A POCSO OFFENCE

Step 1 — Call Police Immediately:
- Dial 100 (Police) or 1098 (CHILDLINE — 24/7 helpline for children in distress)
- Call 1090 (Women Helpline in UP) or 181 (National Women Helpline)
- You can report anonymously if you are afraid

Step 2 — File FIR at the Nearest Police Station:
The police must register an FIR immediately. They must also inform the Child Welfare Committee (CWC) and the SJPU within 24 hours.

Step 3 — Medical Care:
The child must receive immediate medical care. Government hospitals are obligated to provide free medical treatment to POCSO victims.

Step 4 — Child Welfare Committee (CWC):
The CWC (under the Juvenile Justice Act) ensures the child's safety, welfare, and rehabilitation. The CWC may place the child in a safe environment (shelter home or with a trusted relative) pending investigation and trial.

Step 5 — Victim Compensation:
Under the Victim Compensation Scheme (VCS) and POCSO rules, the Special Court can award compensation to the child victim. The State Legal Services Authority manages this fund. Compensation can be awarded even if the accused is acquitted.

REHABILITATION AND SUPPORT

- CHILDLINE (1098): 24/7 helpline for child protection emergencies
- Child Care Institutions: Government-run shelter homes for victims who need temporary accommodation
- Counselling: Mandatory counselling is to be provided to POCSO victims by trained counsellors
- Education continuity: Arrangements are made to ensure the child's education is not disrupted

AGGRAVATED POCSO — THE 2019 AMENDMENT

The POCSO (Amendment) Act, 2019 introduced:
- Death penalty for penetrative sexual assault on children below 12 years
- Enhanced minimum sentences across all categories of offences
- Stricter provisions against child pornography (Sections 14-15 completely revamped)
- Mere storage of child pornography (even without transmission or publication) is now punishable

IMPORTANT NOTE

POCSO offences are among the gravest crimes in Indian law. Every citizen has a duty to protect children and report abuse. If you suspect a child is being abused — at home, in school, by a relative, or online — report it immediately to CHILDLINE (1098) or police (100). Your report can save a child's life and future. All reports are treated confidentially. Free legal aid for POCSO victims is available from the DLSA — contact NALSA helpline 15100. The fight against child sexual abuse begins with reporting.
